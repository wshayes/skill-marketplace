# Objection Handling

Read this for any slide carrying a recommendation. The hardest objection lives on the slide, not in the Q&A.

## Why pre-answer objections

Three reasons:

1. **The audience will raise it anyway.** Withholding the objection doesn't suppress it; it just lets the audience surface it on their terms, in the live discussion, when you have less control.
2. **It signals you have done the thinking.** Audiences trust authors who name their own weak points. The author who only presents the upside is either naive or selling.
3. **It compresses the meeting.** When the objection is already addressed, the audience moves to the next question instead of debating the obvious one. Meetings end with decisions instead of with "let me get back to you."

The author who fears giving the audience ammunition has not understood that the audience already has the ammunition. The choice is whether to address it on the author's terms or on the audience's.

## The pre-mortem

Before composing any recommendation-bearing slide, do this in writing:

1. **State the recommendation in one sentence.**
2. **List the three strongest objections** an intelligent, skeptical reader would raise. Not the easy ones — the ones that make you wince.
3. **Rank them by severity** (likelihood × difficulty to answer).
4. **For the top objection, draft a one-sentence on-slide rebuttal.** If you cannot, the objection is real and the recommendation needs more work — or it needs to be softened to match what the data actually supports.

Common high-severity objections to surface:

- *"Why now?"* — Why this decision in this meeting and not later?
- *"What changed?"* — Why are we revisiting something already decided?
- *"What did you not consider?"* — What's the option not on the slide?
- *"What if you're wrong?"* — What's the cost of acting on a wrong recommendation, vs. the cost of waiting?
- *"Who is accountable?"* — Who owns the outcome if we say yes?
- *"What does this cost us?"* — Cash, time, headcount, opportunity.
- *"Have we done this before?"* — Precedent. If yes, what happened. If no, why think it will work.
- *"Where did this number come from?"* — Source and confidence interval on the key figure.

## On-slide objection patterns

Pick the lightest treatment that handles the objection.

### Pattern 1 — Footnote (lightest)

For objections that can be addressed in one line. Place at the bottom of the slide in smaller type.

> *"Cost estimate assumes a 15% buffer over Pharmaron's Q3 proposal; sensitivity in appendix A2."*

Works when: the objection is real but quantifiable, and the audience will accept the answer if cited.

### Pattern 2 — Inline counter

A single line of body text that names and answers the objection.

> *"This delays IND by four weeks. We accept that delay because the alternative — releasing on incomplete CMC documentation — creates an FDA hold risk we estimate at 30%."*

Works when: the objection is about a trade-off and the recommendation explicitly accepts it.

### Pattern 3 — "Why not X" callout box

A dedicated box on the slide, labelled "Why not [the alternative]?" or "What if [scenario]?", answering the most likely pushback in two to three lines.

Works when: the audience will immediately wonder about a specific alternative or scenario, and the answer doesn't fit in a footnote.

### Pattern 4 — Dedicated objection slide

A whole slide titled with the objection as a question. Use only for the *single hardest* objection in the deck, and only when the answer requires data the audience must see.

> Title: *"Why not delay IND filing by a quarter to clear all CMC gaps?"*
> Body: A comparison of cost, runway impact, and competitive timing. Recommendation restated at the bottom.

Works when: the objection is the meeting. The whole decision turns on it.

## What goes in the appendix vs. on the slide

**On the slide:**
- The objection itself, named explicitly.
- The one-sentence answer.
- A pointer to the appendix if the audience wants to verify.

**In the appendix:**
- The detailed sensitivity analysis.
- The data supporting the answer.
- The dismissed alternatives.

The audience must be able to see *that* the objection was considered without having to navigate to the appendix. Verification belongs in the appendix; awareness belongs on the slide.

## When the objection wins

Sometimes the pre-mortem surfaces an objection you can't answer. This is a feature of the method, not a failure of it. Three responses are honest:

1. **Soften the recommendation** to match what's defensible. ("Recommend X *contingent on Y resolving by Z*" instead of "Recommend X.")
2. **Surface the objection as an open risk** with a proposed mitigation owner. Don't pretend it's answered when it isn't.
3. **Withdraw the recommendation** and ask for a discussion-level slide instead of a decision-level one.

Producing a confident-sounding slide that doesn't survive its own pre-mortem damages the author's credibility on every future deck. The discipline of pre-mortem is partly about which decks not to ship.

## Worked example

**Recommendation:** Approve Option B (license the asset from Vendor X) rather than Option A (build internally).

**Pre-mortem objections:**

1. *"What if Vendor X raises the price after we commit?"*
2. *"We've built before. Why outsource this one?"*
3. *"Vendor X has had two delayed deliveries with their other partners — what's our exposure?"*

**Top objection:** #3 (likelihood high, severity high — directly threatens the timeline that justifies the recommendation).

**On-slide treatment:** A "Why not build?" callout box near the recommendation:

> *"Vendor X's two prior delays were in 2023 under different leadership. Their current ops director joined in Q2 2024; on-time delivery has been 95% since. Contractual penalties (8 weeks of delay → 15% credit) cap our exposure. See A5."*

This pre-answers the objection on the slide, points to verifying data in the appendix, and leaves the audience with one fewer reason to delay the decision.
