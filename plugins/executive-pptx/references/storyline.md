# Storyline Construction

Read this before drafting an action-title outline.

## The storyline IS the deck

A deck without a storyline is a set of slides. A storyline is a logical chain that, read top to bottom in titles alone, takes the audience from where they are to the decision the author needs. Senior audiences read storylines, not slides — they scan titles, decide whether to engage, and ask questions of the slides that interest them.

If the titles read in order don't make a complete argument, the deck doesn't either. No amount of slide-level polish fixes a broken storyline.

## The Pyramid Principle (Minto)

Barbara Minto's structure for analytical communication. Apply top-down:

```
              [Recommendation / Answer]
                       |
       +---------------+---------------+
       |               |               |
  [Reason 1]      [Reason 2]      [Reason 3]
       |               |               |
   [Evidence]     [Evidence]      [Evidence]
```

- **Top of pyramid:** the single sentence the audience must walk away with. This is your first slide.
- **Middle:** three (occasionally four) reasons that support it. These are your section dividers / key-message slides.
- **Bottom:** the evidence for each reason. These are the supporting slides under each key message.

Rules:
- Each level summarises the level below.
- Sibling points are MECE: mutually exclusive (no overlap) and collectively exhaustive (cover the space).
- The pyramid grows downward as you add detail. It does not grow sideways. If you have seven reasons, you have not yet found the three.

## SCQA — for the opening

Used at the start of the deck (and at the start of each section) to set up *why this conversation is happening*:

- **Situation** — the stable, agreed context. The audience nods.
- **Complication** — what changed, broke, or needs deciding. The audience leans in.
- **Question** — the implicit question the Complication raises.
- **Answer** — the recommendation. This is the top of the pyramid.

Example for a CMO oversight deck:

- **S:** Pharmaron is producing MTAI-1025 drug substance for the FIH study.
- **C:** Three pre-approval reviews in Q3 surfaced recurring CMC documentation gaps, including one Critical finding.
- **Q:** Are we comfortable releasing the next batch under current oversight?
- **A:** Recommend a 30-day documentation remediation gate before next release. — *This is slide 1.*

The audience now knows what the deck is for before slide 2 appears.

## BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front

The military / executive convention: the first slide states the ask. Not the agenda. Not the team. Not "thank you for your time." The ask.

Concretely, slide 1 contains:
1. **The recommendation** (one sentence, in the title).
2. **The decision requested** (one sentence, in the body): "We are asking the SteerCo to approve X by Y date."
3. **The two-to-three reasons** (one line each, supporting the title).
4. **The hardest objection, named and pre-answered.**

Everything else is support for slide 1. If slide 1 disappeared, the deck would still need to defend that exact sentence.

## The "So what?" test

For every fact, chart, or bullet, ask: "So what?" Keep asking until you hit the recommendation. If you can't ladder a fact to the recommendation in three "So whats," the fact is decoration and belongs in the appendix or in the cutting-room.

Example:
- Fact: "Aptuit's stability data shows 0.4% degradation at 25°C/60% RH at 6 months."
- So what? "Within ICH Q1A acceptance criteria."
- So what? "Stability supports a 24-month retest period."
- So what? "Confirms suitability for the FIH supply window."
- Connects to: *recommendation that the API is suitable for IND filing.* ✓ Keep.

vs.

- Fact: "Aptuit has 14 years of experience in solid oral dose."
- So what? "They've done this kind of work before."
- So what? "...?"
- No ladder to the recommendation. → Appendix or cut.

## Decision-required tagging

Every slide in the main body carries one of three tags in the top-right corner:

- **DECISION** — the audience is being asked to approve, choose, or commit.
- **DISCUSSION** — the audience is being asked to weigh in but not yet decide.
- **FYI** — context. Use sparingly. If a slide is FYI, justify why it's in the main body and not the appendix.

This tag forces honesty about why each slide exists. Decks where every slide is FYI are status reports masquerading as decision documents.

## Length discipline

- Default main body: **≤10 slides**. Investors and SteerCos generally do not engage with longer decks; they engage with the first 10 and ask the rest as questions.
- Appendix: unlimited, but every appendix slide must be referenced from a main-body slide. Orphan appendix slides are deleted.
- If the storyline doesn't fit in 10, the storyline isn't tight yet. The fix is sharpening, not expanding the budget.

## Appendix discipline

The appendix is for evidence the audience may want to *verify*, not material the author wants to *show*. Practical rules:

- Every appendix slide has a one-line title and exists to answer one specific question.
- Appendix slides are numbered A1, A2, ... and the main deck references them explicitly ("see A3 for the underlying data").
- "Backup" is not a category. If you can't say which question an appendix slide answers, it doesn't belong.

## Closer

The final slide is not "Thank you" or "Questions?" — it is the **ask, restated**, with the next-step owner and date. The audience leaves the room knowing what they just committed to, who owns the follow-up, and when it's due.

## Common storyline failure modes

- **Inverted pyramid.** Background → analysis → recommendation. Senior audiences disengage before slide 5. Flip it: recommendation → reasons → evidence.
- **Two competing recommendations.** Pick one. Demote the other to a fallback option *inside* the main recommendation.
- **No complication.** The audience can't tell why this meeting is happening. Add the C in SCQA.
- **Reasons that don't ladder.** A reason that does not, on its own, support the recommendation is not a reason. It's a fact looking for a home.
- **Evidence promoted to reasons.** If your "three reasons" are actually three pieces of evidence for one reason, you have one reason, not three. That's fine — say so, and find two more genuine reasons or commit to a one-pillar argument.
