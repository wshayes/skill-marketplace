# Action Titles

Read this when drafting or critiquing titles. The title is the most important text on the slide — most of the audience reads nothing else.

## The rule

A title is a complete declarative sentence that states the conclusion of the slide. Not a noun phrase. Not a section header. A sentence.

If you remove the slide body and the title alone, the audience should still know:
1. What the slide is claiming.
2. Why they should care.
3. Roughly what the supporting evidence will be.

## The label → conclusion transformation

The reliable test: would the title fit on a different slide about the same topic? If yes, it's a label.

| Label (wrong) | Conclusion (right) |
|---|---|
| Q3 Performance Overview | Q3 revenue is 12% below target — we need a budget decision |
| Partnership Options | Option B is the lowest-risk path to launch — here's why |
| Market Landscape | Three competitors are converging on our target indication |
| Risks and Mitigations | The IP risk is contained; the supply risk is not |
| Background | The 2024 FDA guidance changes our stability strategy |
| Recommendation | Recommend a 30-day documentation remediation gate before next release |
| Next Steps | We need SteerCo approval to sign the QTA this quarter |
| Financial Summary | Cash runway extends to Q3 2027 — no raise needed before IND |
| Approach | A phased rollout cuts risk by 60% with a 6-week delay |
| Findings | Three Critical findings make this batch unreleasable as-is |
| Project Status | Project is on track for IND filing; one CMC item is the critical path |
| Conclusions | Approve Option B; expect a decision memo within two weeks |

## Patterns that work

- **Quantified claim**: "Q3 revenue is 12% below target..." (number anchors the claim)
- **Comparative**: "Option B beats A on cost and B on time"
- **Causal**: "Three competitors are converging because the FDA guidance opened the door"
- **Asymmetric**: "IP risk is contained; supply risk is not"
- **Recommendation**: "Recommend [action] because [single reason]"
- **Status with implication**: "Project is on track; CMC is the critical path"

Each pattern lands a single sentence that *commits* to a position.

## Patterns to avoid

- **Single noun**: "Background", "Findings", "Risks", "Approach"
- **Topic + colon**: "Q3 Performance: A Closer Look"
- **Vague verbs**: "Exploring our options", "Looking at the landscape", "Understanding the data"
- **Hedged claims**: "Things to consider regarding Option B"
- **Cliffhangers**: "Our path forward" (forward to *where*?)
- **Editorialised filler**: "The exciting opportunity ahead"
- **Stacked qualifiers**: "Preliminary directional analysis suggests possible improvement"

## Length guidance

- **8–14 words** is the working range. Long enough to carry a verb and an object; short enough to be readable in one glance.
- **Two lines maximum.** If your title wraps to three, you have either two ideas or excess qualification — both are fixable.
- **Numbers belong in the title.** "12%", "3 of 5", "two-thirds" — quantification beats adjectives every time.

## Horizontal logic test

Print the deck. Cover everything except titles. Read titles in order.

- Does it form a coherent argument?
- Does each title advance the story, or do some repeat?
- Is there a recommendation? Does it appear early?

If a stranger reading only the titles couldn't summarise the deck's recommendation in one sentence, the titles aren't yet doing their job.

This is the **single most important quality check** for an executive deck. Apply it last, before delivery.

## Title-body alignment

The body of the slide must *prove* the title. A common failure mode is a strong title followed by body content that hedges or wanders. If the body is data, the data must obviously support the title's claim. If the body is text, every bullet must ladder back to the title.

If you rewrite a title to be sharper, re-check the body. The two move together.

## Before/after worked examples

### Example 1 — Stability data slide

**Before:**
- Title: *Stability Data Summary*
- Body: 6-month stability table, multiple conditions, no annotation.

**After:**
- Title: *Six-month stability supports a 24-month retest period at ICH long-term conditions*
- Body: Same table, with the 0.4% degradation row highlighted and a callout: "Below the 1.0% impurity specification."

The data didn't change. The title now commits to a position, and the body proves it.

### Example 2 — Vendor evaluation

**Before:**
- Title: *Vendor Comparison*
- Body: Pros/cons table of three vendors.

**After:**
- Title: *Vendor B wins on cost and timeline; Vendor A wins only on prior relationship*
- Body: Same table, with Vendor B's row visually emphasised and a single-sentence recommendation under the table.

The audience now knows the position the slide is taking before they read a cell.

### Example 3 — Risk slide

**Before:**
- Title: *Risks and Mitigations*
- Body: Five risks with mitigations.

**After:**
- Title: *Two of five risks remain unmitigated — both need SteerCo support to resolve*
- Body: Same table, sorted so the two unmitigated risks are at the top, with the asks called out.

The title surfaces what's actually being requested. The slide stops being informational and starts being a decision driver.

## When you can't write the title

If you can't write a conclusion-title for a slide, that is the signal. It usually means one of:

- The slide doesn't have a single point (split it).
- The point isn't yet defended by the data (gather more, or remove the slide).
- You haven't decided what you think yet (decide before composing the slide).

Don't compose a slide whose title you can't write. Compose the title first, then build the slide to prove it.
