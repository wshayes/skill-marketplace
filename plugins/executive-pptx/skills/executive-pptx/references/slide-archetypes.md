# Slide Archetypes

Use these as the building blocks of an executive deck. Choose an archetype after the storyline is locked, not before.

The principle: **slide form follows slide function.** A slide that asks for a decision is composed differently from a slide that compares options. Picking the wrong archetype is the most common reason slides feel "off" even when the title is good.

## The eight archetypes

| # | Archetype | Function | Decision-tag |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **BLUF / Recommendation** | State the ask up front | DECISION |
| 2 | **Evidence** | Prove one reason with data | DISCUSSION or FYI |
| 3 | **Options comparison** | Choose among MECE alternatives | DECISION |
| 4 | **Trade-off** | Explain what is being given up | DISCUSSION |
| 5 | **Risk & mitigation** | Disclose risks and proposed responses | DISCUSSION |
| 6 | **Status / progress** | Update on commitments previously made | FYI or DISCUSSION |
| 7 | **Asks & decisions** | Closing slide; restate the ask and owners | DECISION |
| 8 | **Appendix** | Verification material | (not tagged) |

## 1. BLUF / Recommendation slide

**Function:** Slide 1 of the deck. States the ask before the audience reads anything else.

**Title:** The recommendation as a complete sentence.

**Body composition:**
- Decision requested (one sentence: who approves what by when).
- 2–3 supporting reasons (one line each).
- One pre-answered objection (callout or footnote).
- A "decision tag" in the corner: DECISION.

**Common failures:** Slide 1 is the agenda. Slide 1 is "About this deck". Slide 1 introduces the team. Move all of these to the appendix or delete.

## 2. Evidence slide

**Function:** Proves one reason from the pyramid with data.

**Title:** The conclusion drawn from the data — not "Data on X" but "X is true because Y."

**Body composition:**
- One primary chart, table, or callout. Not three.
- Annotation directly on the chart showing the key reading (highlight, arrow, callout). The audience should not have to *find* the point.
- A "so-what" line under or beside the chart restating the title's claim in operational terms.

**Data-ink discipline (Tufte):**
- No 3D effects, no decorative gradients, no chartjunk.
- Gridlines only where they aid reading. Drop them if data labels suffice.
- Colour serves emphasis. Grey out the comparison; colour the point.
- If the chart has more than 8 categories, you're either showing the wrong cut or you should aggregate.

## 3. Options comparison slide

**Function:** Force a choice among alternatives.

**Title:** Names the recommended option. "Option B beats A on cost and C on timeline." Never neutral.

**Body composition:**
- Options as columns or rows. **MECE** — no option should overlap with another, and the set should plausibly cover the space (including "do nothing" if relevant).
- Criteria as the other axis. Limit to 3–5 criteria that actually drive the decision.
- Visual emphasis on the recommended option (background fill, border, bold).
- An explicit one-line recommendation under the table. The table alone is an abdication.

**Rule of 3:** Three options is the working sweet spot. Two reads as binary; four-plus reads as a shopping list and dissolves the decision. If you have five options, you have not yet done the work of grouping them.

**The "do nothing" option:** Include it explicitly when status quo is a real alternative. Most option comparisons omit it and accidentally bias the audience toward action.

## 4. Trade-off slide

**Function:** Make explicit what the recommendation gives up.

**Title:** Names both sides. "We accept a 4-week delay to remove FDA hold risk."

**Body composition:**
- The thing gained (column or panel 1).
- The thing given up (column or panel 2).
- Why the gain is worth the cost (single line, prominent).

**Why this archetype exists:** Recommendations always have costs. A deck that hides them invites the audience to discover them, which collapses trust. Surfacing the trade-off is what makes the recommendation credible.

## 5. Risk & mitigation slide

**Function:** Disclose what could go wrong and what's being done about it.

**Title:** Names the asymmetry. "Two of five risks remain unmitigated — both need SteerCo support." Not "Risks and Mitigations."

**Body composition:**
- Risk register table, sorted by severity (highest at top).
- For each risk: likelihood, impact, mitigation, owner, status.
- The unmitigated or partly-mitigated risks visually flagged.
- An explicit ask for any risk requiring leadership action.

**Common failure:** Five-by-five colour-coded heat map with no annotation. Audiences see colour, not content. Pair every heat-map cell with a one-line description, or drop the heat map.

## 6. Status / progress slide

**Function:** Update on commitments previously made. Use when the audience is checking on prior decisions.

**Title:** States the headline status with the asymmetry. "Project is on track; CMC is the critical path." Not "Project Update."

**Body composition:**
- 3–5 workstreams as rows. RAG (red/amber/green) only if you can defend each rating in one sentence.
- For each non-green item: what's needed to return to green, who owns it, by when.
- No "everything is great" green-on-green-on-green tables — they read as performative. Show the one thing that needs attention.

## 7. Asks & decisions slide

**Function:** Final slide of the main body. Closes the loop.

**Title:** Restates the ask. "We are asking for approval of X by Y."

**Body composition:**
- The decision being requested, restated.
- Owner of the next step.
- Date by which the decision is needed.
- Optionally: what happens if no decision is taken (the "default action" — usually unattractive, which is the point).

**Never use:** "Thank you." "Questions?" These waste the most-read slide of the deck. The audience will know to thank you; they will know to ask questions. Use the slide to lock the ask.

## 8. Appendix slides

**Function:** Verification material the audience may want to inspect.

**Rules:**
- Every appendix slide is referenced by name from a main-body slide.
- Slides are numbered A1, A2, ... .
- Each appendix slide has a one-line title that names what question it answers ("A3: Pharmaron stability methodology and ICH alignment").
- Appendix slides do not need to pass the three tests — they exist to be looked up, not read.

## Slide composition rules that span archetypes

### One visual focus per slide
A chart OR a table OR a callout — not all three. If a slide has multiple visual elements, one must dominate (60-70% visual weight) and the others must obviously support it.

### Decision-required tag
Top-right corner of every main-body slide. DECISION / DISCUSSION / FYI. This is not a design preference; it's an honesty check.

### The objection treatment
Every recommendation-bearing slide carries some form of objection treatment — footnote, inline counter, callout box, or dedicated objection slide. See `objection-handling.md`.

### "So what?" footer
Every evidence slide carries a one-line "so what?" — a takeaway line that explicitly connects the data to the recommendation. The data does not speak for itself; the author makes it speak.

### No filler slides
"Agenda", "About us", "Our team", "Thank you", "Questions?" — these don't drive decisions. They either go to the appendix or they go away. Senior audiences do not need agendas; they need answers.
