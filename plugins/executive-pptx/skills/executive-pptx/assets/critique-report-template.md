## Headline finding

One sentence stating the deck-level diagnosis. Examples:

- "The recommendation is buried on slide 7 — move it to slide 1 and the deck becomes deliverable."
- "The titles read as topic labels rather than conclusions; the storyline is not yet legible from the horizontal read-through."
- "The deck is well-structured but lacks objection treatment on the three highest-stakes slides."
- "The deck is ready to ship after two minor edits."

## Deck-level recommendation

Pick one. State why in one sentence.

- [ ] **Ready to ship** — minor polish only.

- [ ] **Restructure** — storyline needs reordering; the slide assets are mostly reusable.
- [ ] **Rebuild** — storyline is not yet defensible; recommend going back to the outline stage before more slide work.

**Severity scale:**
- **Critical** — slide actively undermines the deck (e.g., missing ask, buried recommendation, data that disproves the title).
- **Major** — slide fails one or more of the three tests in a way the audience will notice.
- **Minor** — slide passes the tests but is not yet sharp.
- **Observation** — polish; does not change decision-quality.

## Smallest fix, biggest gain

State the one change that delivers the most improvement. Usually a rewrite of slide 1 plus a closer rewrite. Be specific.

> Example: "Rewriting slide 1's title from 'Q3 Performance Overview' to 'Q3 revenue is 12% below target — we need a budget decision' and adding an Asks & Decisions slide as the new closer will take the deck from FYI to decision-driving without restructuring."

## Open question for the author

Optional — one question that, if answered, would unlock the next round of editing. Examples:

- "What decision do you actually want from this audience? The deck currently asks for two."
- "What's the strongest objection you expect on slide 4? It isn't addressed."
- "Is the appendix budget firm, or can we move three slides out of the main body?"
